# Results Summary (Revised: Additive AKM/KSS Benchmark)

## Editorial Takeaway

1. **Additive AKM/KSS is the first-order mean benchmark.** Persistent organizational heterogeneity explains 45.2% of variance in log output; captain heterogeneity explains 38.4%. The positive sorting covariance (16.4%, Corr = 0.197) indicates modest PAM. This benchmark is robust across specifications (organizational share 45–50%).

2. **Portable routines are identified through behavioral designs on a separate outcome.** Within-captain mover designs show that switching to a higher-capability agent reduces search geometry (Lévy μ), with no pre-trends in event-study specifications. Because the compass effect is on search geometry (not output), it is not vulnerable to the same-outcome interaction critique.

3. **Training-pipeline transmission provides the mechanism.** Promoted mates who continue sailing with their training agent outperform by 0.074 log points. This is the most direct evidence that organizations transmit software (routines, decision rules) rather than merely providing hardware.

4. **Floor-raising is the distinctive distributional result.** Q1 (novice) captains benefit roughly 2× more from organizational capability than Q4 (expert) captains. High-ψ organizations compress outcome variance by 56% for novices.

5. **Matching operates on two margins.** Under the additive AKM in logs, mean-output-maximizing assignment in levels is PAM (multiplicative separability). But the floor-raising result implies a second, risk-management margin: allocating organizational capability toward weaker captains reduces left-tail risk, at the cost of lower mean output.

## What This Paper Does NOT Claim

- It does **not** claim that the production function is globally submodular. The θ×ψ interaction (old Table 5, now Appendix A3) is suggestive supplementary evidence, not the main structural model.
- It does **not** claim that "captains own the map." Raw Shannon MI is inflated by high-cardinality bias; AMI and conditional MI are the corrected objects.
- It does **not** claim that high-ψ agents universally "fail fast." The stopping rule operates adaptively, with threshold-dependent discipline.

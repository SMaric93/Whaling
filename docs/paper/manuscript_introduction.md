# Introduction

## 1.1 Motivation

What do organizations do? A large and growing literature in strategy, organizational economics, and labor economics documents persistent heterogeneity in firm productivity, but the mechanisms through which organizations create value beyond the sum of their employees remain difficult to identify. The fundamental challenge is empirical: separating genuine organizational capability from selection — the tendency of better organizations to attract better workers — and from confounding through shared technology, geography, or market conditions.

We study this question in a setting that offers unusually transparent identification: the American offshore whaling industry, 1807–1922. Whaling voyages were organized by managing agents who selected captains, provisioned vessels, and — we argue — shaped the search routines that captains followed at sea. The setting provides a natural two-sided matching market between captains and agents, with productivity directly observed as oil output per voyage. Critically, captains frequently switched between managing agents over their careers, providing the mover variation that identifies additive fixed effects in the AKM (Abowd, Kramarz, and Margolis 1999) framework.

## 1.2 Contributions

This paper makes three contributions.

**First, we benchmark mean productivity using an additive AKM/KSS variance decomposition.** Applying the leave-one-out connected set procedure with empirical Bayes shrinkage and KSS (Kline, Saggio, and Sølvsten 2020) bias correction, we find that persistent organizational heterogeneity accounts for 65.4% of explained log-output variance, while persistent captain heterogeneity accounts for 33.0%, with negligible sorting (Corr(θ,ψ) = 0.02). The organizational share remains at 60–70% across specifications that progressively add vessel controls and richer fixed-effect structures (Table A1). This additive benchmark is the appropriate first-order model because it avoids the circularity of generated-regressor interaction designs, where estimated fixed effects are used as regressors in a second-stage production function.

**Second, we identify portable organizational routines through behavioral designs on a separate outcome.** Within-captain mover designs show that switching to a higher-capability agent alters the captain's search geometry (Lévy exponent μ), even after absorbing route-by-time fixed effects and hardware controls (Table 4). Event-study estimates show no pre-trends and a discrete shift at the switch point (Table 5). Because the compass effect is estimated on a behavioral outcome (search geometry) distinct from the production outcome (oil output), it is not vulnerable to the same-outcome interaction critique. We further trace the transmission channel through training pipelines: promoted mates perform significantly better (0.279 log points, t = 3.28) with the agent who originally trained them (Table 6), providing the most direct evidence that organizations transmit portable software rather than merely providing time-invariant hardware.

**Third, we show that organizational assignment has two margins — mean-allocation and risk-allocation — and that the risk margin is quantitatively important.** Conditional average treatment effects estimated via CausalForestDML reveal that novice captains (Q1) benefit 6× as much from organizational capability as expert captains (Q4) (CATE = 1.194 vs. 0.201), with organizational capability compressing outcome variance by 76% for novices (Table 7, Table A5). This floor-raising pattern has dramatic extensive-margin consequences: high-capability organizations reduce the zero-catch rate for novice captains from 82% to 16% (Table 8, Panel B). Under the additive AKM benchmark in logs, the mean-output-maximizing assignment in levels is positive assortative matching (PAM), since production is multiplicatively separable. But the distinctive organizational value appears in the tails: stronger organizations stabilize weak captains rather than amplifying the output of already-strong captains.

## 1.3 Relationship to the Literature

Our paper contributes to several literatures. In organizational economics and strategy, it complements studies of persistent firm effects (Syverson 2011; Bloom et al. 2019) by providing clean identification of organizational capability through mover designs in a pre-modern labor market with transparent matching. In labor economics, it applies the AKM framework (Abowd, Kramarz, and Margolis 1999; Card, Heining, and Kline 2013) to a historical two-sided market and demonstrates that the employer premium operates through behavioral governance rather than purely compensating differentials. In the matching literature, it extends the Becker (1973) assignment framework by showing that assignment has a second, risk-management margin that can dominate the mean-output margin for weaker workers.

Our descriptive evidence on route-choice information is corrected by adjusted mutual information (AMI) and conditional mutual information. Raw Shannon MI attributed 83.5% of route-choice information to captain identity, but this figure is inflated by high-cardinality finite-sample bias. AMI reduces captain dominance from 3.84 bits to 0.114 bits (Table 3, Panel A), while conditional MI reveals that captains retain substantial idiosyncratic routing knowledge conditional on organizational assignment (I(G;Captain|Agent) = 1.471 vs. I(G;Agent|Captain) = 0.546; Table 3, Panel B). The corrected information tables, not the raw Shannon figures, form the basis for claims about the locus of routing knowledge.

No lay or incentive contract variables survive in the analytic repository (Table A10). Institutional evidence (Davis, Gallman & Gleiter 1997) indicates lay shares were standardized by rank, port, and era, limiting their ability to drive the organizational heterogeneity we document. The lay-system alternative remains plausible but cannot be directly estimated with the available data.
